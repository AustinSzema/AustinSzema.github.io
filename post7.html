How can you build a system that turns talented individuals into industry ready professionals?

Austin Szema

4/23/26

I want to make a factory that takes talented individuals who have the hard skills necessary for game development, but lack the soft skills, industry connections, networking abilities, and general job related necessities to truly thrive, and provide them with those necessary skills.

The first part of this would require some sort of connection to an established program with a good reputation or some other method of credibility. This could be either a university or a company founded by industry veterans and professionals.

Once that has been established, you would need some method of recruiting people to join this program, but ensuring that the quality of the accepted applicants is exceptional. The ideal candidate is an individual who graduated from a university of high caliber, who then tried to get into the industry without the proper networking or skills, and are now desperate and looking for a means of acquiring those skills. This desperation fuels passion and intrinsic motivation. If they have tried and failed, they will understand the challenges that they need to overcome, and if they are still willing and making an effort to stay in the fight and overcome those challenges, then they will be a far more ideal candidate than an individual who has tried, failed, and given up.

Without intrinsic motivation, people cannot be taught because they will simply refuse to accept the value of what is being taught.

Once accepted, the batch of talent must be provided with a significant enough value proposition to justify their time and effort in the program. Ideally, success in this program would result in a guaranteed job offer, either by the company running the program, or by means of networking connections.

This also means that the batch of talent must face immense challenges, harsh yet constructive criticism of their work, and have their LinkedIn, Resume, Portfolio, and general presentation skills fully reworked to be suitable for the industry. They must also have a way to connect with the wider industry as a whole.

This program would not be a place to learn the hard skills of how to be the best 3D artist for example. They should come in already knowing 3D Art, but lacking the skills necessary to demonstrate their talents and acquire a job.

Many people fail to realize that the job hunting market is a game. It is a game of networking, pitching yourself, understanding the language of recruiters, and demonstrating why you are not just valuable to the company but a NECESSITY.

Resumes, Portfolios, and LinkedIn profiles are living entities. They are not static nor fixed, and they are never “done”. As new projects emerge and are written into your history as a developer, these documents MUST CHANGE AND GROW.

The exchange that must occur, is that the talent subscribes their success to the teachings of the program, and thus feels obligated to support it and maintain its quality standards for future generations of talent. This builds an alumni network that strengthens over time exponentially, as new graduates are hired by former graduates, thus infinitely growing the network of the overall program.

What I have just described is essentially what the ETC does.

There is immense opportunity to create thriving new sectors of the game industry by creating more programs like the ETC in locations outside of Pittsburgh. Poaching the talent of places like NYC, Boston, and Florida, and building up a massive network of alumni, would make not only the strength of the program better, but the strength of industry better, and for the individual who created it all, a powerful network that dominates the industry.
