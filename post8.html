Not Quite My Framerate

Austin Szema

4/30/26

"Meh." This is the word used by my mentor that has been one of the most powerful and impactful parts of my game design career.

As part of a summer internship where we published a mobile game (MassDiGI), we were required to pitch several ideas to our manager and mentor, and if it wasn't good enough, he would respond with "Meh." More people need to hear this. More people need to be told their ideas are worthless, or more precisely, worth $0.00833. An idea only has any real value if you are actually able to execute on it.

There is no such thing as a "finished game". There are only games that ship and games that don't ship. The hardest part of game development is that last 10%, getting it out the door and into the public eye.

These are some of the lessons that I have learned throughout my game design journey, from various internships, classes, online videos, and talking with other developers. I want to imbue these lessons upon future game developers, but the problem is that few people want to be told these hard truths.

There exists a gap in games education, on which lies in it's very foundation: education. Universities are businesses. They are there to make money, and students pay a lot of money. Due to the structure of how many educational institutions work, it is disadvantageous for them to fail people, whether that be for tuition money, school image, statistics, or the potential backlash against professors. This results in a deep flaw that leads to people who put the bare minimum of effort into graduating with a degree. The problem is that people are able to do just enough to pass, and to graduate without ever having done any actual work. "Oh I'm the producer (but doesn't actually do any producing)" "Oh I'm a designer (on a team of 6 people that already has 4 other designers)".

There needs to be a structure in which people are allowed to fail. Failure is our greatest learning tool, it has been for all of existence. Evolution is the process of filtering out those who failed to adapt. Through failure, we learn, grow, change, and do better than we did before. Knowledge is the spread of the lessons learned from the cummulative failure of others, so as to prevent those mistakes from coming about again.

Therefore I propose this: A gaunlet in which game designers are put through a series of challenges that forces them to fail and learn. You might think this sounds similar to the CMU ETC BVW (building virtual worlds) class, but the big difference here is that not everyone will make it to the next semester. In fact, more than half of the participants shouldn't.

This gauntlet would be structured similarly to a typical reality competition series like Hell's Kitchen. Contestants compete in a series of challenges designed to put them under intense pressure and difficult situations. This could be game jams, design challenges, teamwork challenges, etc. One specific idea would be to have a game jam where halfway through, a new design constraint is added in, after the team has already done a lot of design work. This would be to simulate the real world, in which a higher up corporate decision has been made, which requires the team to pivot and adapt. If a team cannot be flexible enough to adapt to a rising challenge, what kind of game designers are we producing?

In order to run this show, you would need someone with immense experience in the games industry, with knowledge of all the disciplines, and with enough grit to tell people their ideas and their work is BAD. This is one of the biggest flaws with the games education system, is that not enough people are told that their ideas are BAD. The world does not need more 2D platformer metroidvania roguelikes.

After having tried and failed, they will understand the challenges that they need to overcome, and if they are still willing and making an effort to stay in the fight and overcome those challenges, then they will be a far more ideal candidate than an individual who has tried, failed, and given up. The successful individuals who have proven that they have the motivation and drive to actually put in the work and obtain the hard skills necessary for game development, will now move on to the second phase. Anyone not having shown their willingness to achieve their dreams shall be removed from the program. Without intrinsic motivation, people cannot be taught because they will simply refuse to accept the value of what is being taught.

The successful individuals would then be trained in the soft skills. Soft skills like networking, making industry connections, and general job related necessities that allow them to truly thrive.

This second phase does not mean the end of challenge or harsh criticism though, they must now have their LinkedIn, Resume, Portfolio, and general presentation skills painfully scrutinized and reworked to be suitable for the industry.

Many people fail to realize that the job hunting market is a game. It is a game of networking, pitching yourself, understanding the language of recruiters, and demonstrating why you are not just valuable to the company but a NECESSITY.

Resumes, Portfolios, and LinkedIn profiles are living entities. They are not static nor fixed, and they are never “done”. As new projects emerge and are written into your history as a developer, these documents MUST CHANGE AND GROW.

After having obtained the necessary skills, the most thriving candidates would be sent out to actual industry locations to work under the scrutiny of a real company. The expectations would be high, but with the training they have received, it should be more than achievable.

The intensity of this program would yield an extremely low batch of graduates, something that many educational institutions would fear, but the quality of those candidates would be absolutely exceptional, and in due time, would strengthen the industry immensely.
