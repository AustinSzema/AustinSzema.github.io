What Expedition 33 Means for the Game Industry

Austin Szema

2/5/26

Clair Obscur: Expedition 33 swept the game awards this year, sparking lots of debate online for several different reasons. Many people called into question the game being defined as an “indie game”, as its graphical fidelity and financial backing puts it more in the category of a AA game, not an indie game. I think the most interesting thing about this game is not the game itself, but the story behind the studio, and what it means for the future of the industry. Sandfall Interactive was founded by an ex-Ubisoft developer who grew frustrated with the lack of creative freedom that comes with AAA development. The core team consists of roughly 33 game industry veterans who came from primarily AAA backgrounds with a lot of them being ex-Ubisoft, however the team also outsourced lots of the work to external contractors, a large part of why many people questioned the game’s “indie” nature.

The narrative that has been perpetuated around this studio “33 ex-Ubisoft devs founded a studio and made game of the year”, while not entirely accurate, tells a broader story about the current state of the industry. Developers are increasingly becoming frustrated with the creative restrictions and lack of innovation that many AAA companies face due to their financial and organizational structures, with many developers waiting to see which of their colleagues will be the first to make the jump to indie. With the release and critical acclaim of Expedition 33, a signal flare has been sent out to the industry that times are changing. You no longer need a massive corporation in order to make a high fidelity, financially successful, and critically acclaimed game. If a group of ex-AAA developers with the help of outsourced external developers can make Game of the Year with their very first release, then so can others. I anticipate that within the next 5-10 years, we will start to see a massive shift in the industry, as experienced developers depart from their AAA studios, taking their friends with them, and starting their own studios with the help of outsourcing to make the next Game of the Year. Part of this shift comes with the fact that you no longer need to be “a tiny indie game”. The jump doesn’t have to be so drastic that you go from a AAA studio to a fully “indie” studio, you can go from AAA to AA, using the financial backings of your industry connections to support development, with your investors knowing that your team has the experience and technical ability to deliver a successful product.

We have reached a point in the games industry where some of the earliest pioneers of the industry have ascended to senior director roles. We have entered into a period where the industry has become saturated with tons of well seasoned developers who may be looking for new opportunities. We are also the first wave of industry veterans who will be retiring soon. Many people fail to realize how new the game industry is, with it having started around the 1970s, it is now beginning to mature and see its first wave of original developers start to retire. Many of the legends of industry are around the ages of 50-70 (Todd Howard, John Carmac, and Masahiro Sakurai are all 55 as of today 2/5/26) and as they approach retirement age, we will begin to see a huge shift in the industry, as this will be the first time that these long-standing names will no longer be active in the space, leaving opportunity for new legends to be made. On the CMU ETC west coast trip this year, we visited many studios in LA and SF, and a common thread amongst many of these up and coming studios was the same story: a group of ex-AAA developers started a studio and are working on the next big franchises. These studios share many familiarities with Sandfall Interactive, AAA background, not quite indie but not AAA, and tapping into the ideas of their seasoned veteran leaders, with total creative freedom.

Most of these studios are only hiring Senior talent as of right now, but as they grow, the need to hire new talent to keep their companies sustained will arise, which bodes positively for the thousands of students looking to break into the industry in the next generation.
